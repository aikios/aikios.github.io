Gedicht

She looked over his shoulder
For vines and olive trees,
Marble well-governed cities
And ships upon untamed seas,
But there on the shining metal
His hands had put instead
An artificial wilderness
And a sky like lead.
A plain without a feature, bare and brown,
No blade of grass, no sign of neighborhood,
Nothing to eat and nowhere to sit down,
Yet, congregated on its blankness, stood
An unintelligible multitude,
A million eyes, a million boots in line,
Without expression, waiting for a sign.
Out of the air a voice without a face
Proved by statistics that some cause was just
In tones as dry and level as the place:
No one was cheered and nothing was discussed;
Column by column in a cloud of dust
They marched away enduring a belief
Whose logic brought them, somewhere else, to grief.
She looked over his shoulder
For ritual pieties,
White flower-garlanded heifers,
Libation and sacrifice,
But there on the shining metal
Where the altar should have been,
She saw by his flickering forge-light
Quite another scene.
Barbed wire enclosed an arbitrary spot
Where bored officials lounged (one cracked a joke)
And sentries sweated for the day was hot:
A crowd of ordinary decent folk
Watched from without and neither moved nor spoke
As three pale figures were led forth and bound
To three posts driven upright in the ground.
The mass and majesty of this world, all
That carries weight and always weighs the same
Lay in the hands of others; they were small
And could not hope for help and no help came:
What their foes like to do was done, their shame
Was all the worst could wish; they lost their pride
And died as men before their bodies died.
She looked over his shoulder
For athletes at their games,
Men and women in a dance
Moving their sweet limbs
Quick, quick, to music,
But there on the shining shield
His hands had set no dancing-floor
But a weed-choked field.
A ragged urchin, aimless and alone,
Loitered about that vacancy; a bird
Flew up to safety from his well-aimed stone:
That girls are raped, that two boys knife a third,
Were axioms to him, who'd never heard
Of any world where promises were kept,
Or one could weep because another wept.
The thin-lipped armorer,
Hephaestos, hobbled away,
Thetis of the shining breasts
Cried out in dismay
At what the god had wrought
To please her son, the strong
Iron-hearted man-slaying Achilles
Who would not live long.

The Shield of Achilles - W.H. Auden

The Shield of Achilles is ostensibly an ekphrastic poem, describing the titular shield as forged by Hephaestos in the thick of the Trojan War for the wrathful avenger Achilles. However, on closer examination, the text becomes an admonition to war hawks, juxtaposing horrific and wretched imagery with traditional war glorification in an attempt to warn the naive about the real nature of fighting.

In the first stanza, we are introduced to the only two important characters in this poem. Their names are not revealed nor relevant until the final chapter. Those familiar with Homer’s Iliad, however, will recognize from the title that the woman in question is Thetis, mother of Achilles, and the smith laboring on the shield is Hephaestos. The first stanza is the “call”, the first expectation in a series of comparisons made throughout the poem. Thetis expects to see idealistic scenes, much like those sung of in the epic poems of the time: symbols of nature’s gift of life, testaments to the reason, logic and bravery of man. Instead, she sees a bleak, leaden sky.

In the second stanza, Thetis’ hopes are dashed. Hephaestos’ dull engraving is one of monotony and pain. An endless plain is summoned to mind, one without a “blade of grass” or a “sign of neighborhood”. The governed city of marble is replaced with a vast army, blurred and nameless in its magnitude. The use of words like “congregated”, “unintelligible”, “multitude”, and “million” help to both conjure an image of desolation, but also to make clear that we are, in fact, looking at an army on a battlefield. This is an important choice by Auden, one that helps the reader immediately understand what the poem is talking about: war.

The third stanza presents a chronological follow up to the second: the voice of someone, presumably a general, calls out instructions for the army, and justifications for their fight. However, unlike the heroic rallying cries of yore, this one falls upon deafened, dull ears: the soldiers just march away. They are described as having “endured” the ideology that the general promotes: Auden’s wording contributes to the image of soldiers who don’t know what they fight for, only that they must fight in some never-ending war. The phrase “proved by statistics” lends to this portrayal as well by showing that the war is not fought by inflamed passions or for glory: someone somewhere else has decided that the fight must take place, and so the banner is carried.

In the presentation of the second and third stanza, we also see a contrast in structure. The first stanza, and the fourth and the sixth after, representative of the hopes of Thetis, are all made of short and flowing lines, evocative of dreams and the vague ideals chased by the yet-innocent. The stanzas of Hephaestos (the rest) contain lines that are longer and more choppy, presenting a slog of a read to the reader as well as eliciting the slow, tired rhythm of war and reality. This structure extends to the rest of the poem.

The fourth stanza harkens back to the first: we imagine Thetis looking further on this shield, beautiful scenes of sacrifice that bring to mind the Grecian urn lauded by Keats. The idea of a ritual piety brings to mind the ancient nature of war and the battlefield as a coming-of-age for young men, told to return carrying their shield or being carried on it. The white cow being led to the pedestal as a sacrifice to the gods is a metaphor for the innocence of youth, corrupted and blindly led towards a horrible fate for the promise of a vague ideal. The theme of sacrifice and purity is especially relevant given the context of the scene as taking place in the Trojan War: the warriors fight for the gods, blessed by rival factions as sacrifices, running at each other on some divine chessboard. Achilles comes to bear this shield as he enters the fighting because of the loss of his dear Patroclus, an innocent, glory-seeking youth who was stirred up by the fervor and ran headlong into battle, unaware of the gory reality that would follow.

However, instead of this pure and ideal sacrifice, in the fifth and sixth stanzas, we see a much more gritty reality carved by Hephaestos into the shield: three figures, presumably prisoners of war, are led into a barbed wire enclosure and crucified on posts, much to the indifference of the guards standing watch. They too form a sacrifice to parallel the “flower-garlanded heifer”: they represent the dark, painful side of war, the soldiers who couldn’t be the prodigal heroes they thought they would be hailed as. They are the soldiers that were led to the altar of foreign theater by their country, and forgotten in the name of the greater good. The inclusion of the phrase “one cracked a joke” further contributes to the sense of desolation and misery: these men could not even die an honorable death, like how King Priam prostrated himself at Achilles’ feet for the body of his son. The symbolism of the “ordinary decent folk” is one of the forgetting population, of the nation they fought for, the one that will know of their fight, but forget what it stood for. The sixth stanza is a little more abstract: it talks in vague terms of the hopelessness that these men, and soldiers in general, face. Their death as men before the death of their bodies is a powerful commentary on the depersonalization present in war today: there are no heroes and their legions, no Achilles and his Myrmidons: only positions and numbers. Concepts like these, as well as the mention of barbed wire, which was not invented until the 19th century, indicates that Auden is blending time periods in his work: the epic idealism is the type of ancient societies, while the gritty realism reflects the world of today, with emotionless and detached battle.

The seventh stanza is perhaps one of the most interesting, because it is the one that veers sharply from reality. Thetis looks over the shoulder of Hephaestos, expecting to see a scene of athletic games and dancing. In Homer’s own text, the shield is described as portraying, among 8 other scenes, a scene of men and women dancing upon a dancing-floor. However, Auden chooses to instead put on the shield a weed-choked field: a barren, wretched antithesis to the original idea.

In this desolate lot, we are led to imagine an urchin, “aimless and alone”. Auden makes use of wordplay by positing the opposite: the youth makes a “well-aimed” attack with a stone on a bird, only to have the bird fly out of reach. The relative experience suggested by this imagery indicates a long period of ruin, because of which young children have to hunt their dinners in the wilderness. The youth takes as given occurrences of life the crimes of rape and murder which are described next, further contributing to the sense of a bleak future brought on by war. His world is not one of promises kept or emotions shown: these ideals were presumably eroded by constant war and strife, and suitably round out the stark portrait of war that Auden portrays through the vignettes on the shield.

In the final stanza, we see the lame Hephaestos walk away from his masterwork, carved with his godly knowledge of the reality behind war. Thetis cries out because of the horror of it all; the “shining breasts” Auden writes of represents the boundless hope, optimism, and naivete that this mother possesses for her bloodthirsty child. The final two lines present one last juxtaposition: Achilles is described as “iron-hearted” and “man-slaying”, truly a formidable warrior. But this is not to last, because war is what war is: a place where men go to die. Achilles will “not live long”, despite the love of Thetis which made him almost invulnerable (his baptism in the river Styx): the gods will engineer a hero in Paris to surmount the insurmountable, guiding the arrow to the weak spot, the chink in armor that separates men from gods,
and so the war rages on.
